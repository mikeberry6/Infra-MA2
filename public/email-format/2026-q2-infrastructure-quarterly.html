01  Deal Activity Monitor
Infrastructure deal activity started the year strong, with global announced M&A exceeding $110bn across 100+ transactions. Momentum was led by power and renewables as investors continued to favor scaled platforms and generation portfolios tied to decarbonization and grid reliability.
Headline power transactions included the $33.4bn sale of AES Corporation, the $6.5bn sale of Boralex, and sizeable gas-fired portfolio transactions including Constellation Energy's $5.0bn 4.4GW portfolio sale, Cogentrix Energy's $4.0bn 5.5GW portfolio sale, and ECP's $3.5bn 2.6GW portfolio sale.
Beyond power, transport was a supporting theme, with conviction across large-scale logistics and mobility infrastructure, including Qube's $8.3bn sale, Macquarie AirFinance's $7.0bn sale, United Ports' $2.4bn stake sale, and TCR Group's $1.3bn sale.
Implication: activity is broad enough to support a constructive market, but the highest-conviction capital is clustering around scaled power and transport assets where strategic relevance is clear.
02  Macro Spotlight
Headline risk has returned, but the bar for a material slowdown remains high.
The backdrop in early 2026 remains constructive, with solid underlying growth and generally supportive financial conditions. At the same time, geopolitical tensions and higher oil prices are introducing greater headline volatility and uncertainty around the path of growth and inflation.
As outlined in Guggenheim Investments' Q1 2026 report, Quarterly Macro Themes, the U.S. economy continues to demonstrate resilience in the face of external shocks. The conflict with Iran and the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz drove oil prices up over 47%, sparking supply-chain and inflation concerns, but the broader impact is buffered by declining U.S. energy intensity.
Core inflation remains at sustainable levels, rising just 2.5% year over year in February, while five-year forward inflation expectations are anchored near 2.0%. Guggenheim Investments maintains a positive 12-month outlook, with the Federal Reserve anticipated to maintain an easing bias and fiscal policy providing a tailwind for capital-intensive sectors.
03  Sector Spotlight
Power deal momentum deepens as the sector dominates North American infrastructure M&A activity.
Strategic dealmaking across the infrastructure landscape reached unprecedented concentration in early 2026. In the first quarter, of the nine North American infrastructure deals featuring a disclosed transaction value over $500mm, eight were explicitly focused on power and renewables.
This concentration reflects the critical reality that reliable, scalable generation capacity has become a prerequisite for the power demands of the digital infrastructure boom. Investors are aggressively acquiring heavy-asset power platforms capable of delivering firm load.
Implication: power is no longer a narrow energy transition theme; it is becoming the enabling layer for digital infrastructure, industrial load, and reliability-driven capital deployment.
04  Product Spotlight
LP appetite has returned, but infrastructure fundraising is becoming a barbell market.
Infrastructure has firmly established itself as the most resilient private market asset class, recently crossing $1.6tn in global AUM following a record ~$200bn fundraising year in 2025. Propelled by secular mandates, LPs have decisively increased their infrastructure allocations.
The fundraising landscape is defined by a flight to scale and the pursuit of operational alpha. Nearly three-quarters of new capital flowed to the top 50 mega-funds, while approximately 70% of raised funds targeted core-plus / value-add strategies.
The product pipeline into mid-2026 appears strong. Open-ended funds now comprise roughly 10% of the market, while average hold periods have increased to 7.6 years. Smooth execution for upcoming vintages will depend on stabilizing macroeconomic conditions.
Implication: LP appetite remains intact, but capital formation is concentrating around managers that can pair scale with operational value creation and differentiated sourcing.
05  Financing Market
Financing remains available for infrastructure, but terms are increasingly sorting by asset quality, narrative risk, and sponsor credibility.
Leveraged credit markets entered 2026 with solid momentum, but sentiment softened in February - initially driven by AI disruption concerns, with pressure subsequently increasing from the war with Iran, persistent inflation, pre-existing stress in credit portfolios, and rising investor redemption requests.
In syndicated markets, primary issuance has declined, though higher-quality credits insulated from AI risk remain well-received by investors. Consistent CLO creation suggests ample dry powder is sidelined to support new deal execution as volatility subsides.
In private credit, new issue spreads are estimated to have widened 50-100 bps given the market volatility. Pricing for high-quality sponsor-backed unitranches has moved from S+450 entering 2026 to S+500 or wider today, with investors shifting focus from sourcing opportunities to managing portfolio pressure.
