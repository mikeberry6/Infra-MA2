Weekly Briefing Materials

Beneficial Reuse / Organics Infrastructure Thesis

Investor-focused briefing pages on why residual management becomes infrastructure, how to size the investable market, and where durable value accrues.

Page 1 – Core Thematics: Why This Is Infrastructure

Why Beneficial Reuse / Organics Is Infrastructure

Core message: This is not a sustainability theme or a commodity theme. It becomes infrastructure when recurring residual liabilities require scarce physical assets, permits, local logistics, and contracted compliance services.

Suggested layout: Top: one-sentence thesis. Middle: infrastructure-thematic wheel or matrix. Bottom: what this means for Investor.

Infrastructure Thesis Equation

Recurring residual liability

  • Local logistics / feedstock control
  • Scarce permitted processing capacity
  • Contracted service-fee revenue
  • Compliant beneficial-use outlet

= infrastructure-style opportunity

Infrastructure Thematic Why It Matters
Essential service Food waste, biosolids, FOG, manure, and industrial residuals must be managed continuously.
Inelastic supply WWTPs, restaurants, food processors, farms, and municipalities generate residuals every day.
Local monopoly traits Transport radius, odor buffers, route density, and permitting create local defensibility.
Regulatory pull Organics mandates, PFAS pressure, wastewater rules, methane policy, and grease compliance force action.
Scarce assets Compost sites, depackagers, dryers, tank farms, digesters, land banks, and interconnects are hard to replicate.
Contracted revenue base Tipping, treatment, hauling, O&M, municipal contracts, and P3 payments can support infra underwriting.
Multiple monetization channels Service fees, product revenue, RNG, renewable-fuel credits, and compliance value can stack.
Investor should underwrite the bottleneck, not the output narrative.
The investable question is: who controls the ton, who owns the permitted node, and who gets paid to make the residual problem go away?

Page 2 – Landscape of Feedstock and TAM

Feedstock Landscape: Large Physical TAM, Smaller Investable TAM

Core message: The residual universe is large, but only a subset is collectible, contractable, processable, and monetizable. Investor should focus on bankable residual control, not headline tonnage.

Suggested layout: Left: feedstock landscape table. Right: funnel from gross TAM to investable TAM.
Feedstock Family Examples Core Investable Angle
Food waste / SSO Residential scraps, commercial food waste, grocery shrink Collection, depackaging, compost, AD
Industrial organics F&B residuals, high-BOD liquids, packaged food slurry Liquid logistics, pretreatment, AD, WRRF co-digestion
Yard / green waste Leaves, brush, clean wood, tree trimmings Compost, mulch, soil products, bulking agent
Biosolids / septage Municipal sludge, dewatered cake, septage Contracted utility residuals management
FOG / UCO / grease Trap grease, UCO, yellow / brown grease Pretreatment, rendering, RD / SAF feedstocks
Animal byproducts Tallow, offal, meat byproducts, proteins Rendering, feed, pet food, renewable fuel inputs
Manure / ag residuals Dairy / hog manure, bedding, separated solids RNG, nutrients, bedding, digestate management
Landfill gas Methane from legacy disposal Adjacent RNG / power infrastructure

Investable TAM Funnel

Funnel Stage What It Means
Gross residual pool All physical residuals generated.
Collectible tons Dense enough to aggregate economically.
Contractable tons Secured by MSAs, franchises, municipal contracts, farm agreements, or utility obligations.
Processable tons Compatible with permits, technology, contamination limits, and outlet specs.
Monetizable beneficial-use tons Outputs have real buyers or compliant acceptance: compost, RNG, fertilizer, feed, fats, soil products, or disposal alternative.
Gross waste volume is not the market. Contractable, processable, outlet-secured residual flow is the market.

Page 3 – What Drives Durable / Infra-Like Investment Value

Durable Value Drivers: Where the Moat Lives

Core message: Value accrues to businesses that control scarce local bottlenecks: routes, permits, preprocessing, land banks, interconnects, compliance capabilities, and offtake.

Suggested layout: Two-column page: left side value drivers, right side what diligence must prove.
Value Driver What It Looks Like Diligence Focus
Feedstock control Routes, franchises, exclusive generator MSAs, utility contracts, farm rights Contract term, exclusivity, termination rights, volume minimums
Permitted asset scarcity Compost site, depackager, dryer, AD, rendering plant, tank farm, land bank Permit capacity, feedstock approvals, transferability, expansion rights
Route density Dense organics, FOG, UCO, biosolids, or liquid-waste routes Stops per route, customer churn, labor / fuel cost, density synergies
Contracted base revenue Tipping, hauling, treatment, O&M, municipal service fees Indexation, counterparty quality, minimum volume, rebid risk
Outlet certainty Compost buyers, land application, digestate outlets, RNG interconnect, fuel / feed offtake Product specs, buyer depth, fallback outlets, seasonal constraints
Compliance capability PFAS testing, odor control, stormwater, pathogen reduction, chain-of-custody NOV history, indemnities, testing protocols, liability allocation
Multiple revenue legs Service fee + product + energy + credits Base case without merchant credit upside
Platform scalability Tuck-ins, shared compliance, centralized maintenance, cross-selling Regional density, management depth, systems maturity

Revenue Stack Graphic

Underwrite as core Municipal contracts, collection fees, treatment fees, tipping, O&M
Underwrite conservatively Compost, mulch, fertilizer, tallow, feed ingredients, RNG / power
Treat as upside unless contracted RINs, LCFS, 45Z, RECs, carbon credits
Best assets clear the downside case on service fees and contracted physical economics. Credits and commodities should improve returns, not create the returns.

How Investor Should Read the Strips

Strip Type Underwriting Treatment
Contracted service-fee strips Core infrastructure cash flow
Processing / logistics strips Operational value creation
Product / commodity strips Conservative upside case
Credit / policy strips Upside unless hedged or fixed

The best platform is not the one with the highest theoretical output value. It is the one with the most defensible base strips and the most valuable upside strips.
